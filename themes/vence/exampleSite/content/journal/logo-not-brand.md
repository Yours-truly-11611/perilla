We say this to clients early, because it saves time later: your logo is not your brand.

Your brand is the sum of every experience someone has with your organisation — every interaction, every piece of communication, every moment of service or failure. The logo is a symbol that points toward all of that. It's the signal, not the signal's meaning.

This distinction matters enormously for how you think about design investment.

## What a logo can and cannot do

A well-designed logo can be distinctive, legible, and appropriate. It can avoid looking like your competitors. It can hold up across applications and scale. These are real virtues, and not trivially achieved.

What it cannot do is make people trust you, love you, or choose you over an alternative. Those things are built through the actual experience of your product, your people, your communication, your behaviour over time. The logo is how people recognise the thing — it doesn't constitute the thing.

## The rebrand trap

Companies facing a difficult period often reach for a rebrand. It's visible, controllable, and communicates action. And sometimes the brand genuinely is the problem — an identity that no longer fits what the company has become, or that was never quite right to begin with.

But more often, the brand is a proxy for something harder to solve: a product problem, a culture problem, a positioning problem. A new logo won't fix those. It might briefly distract from them. The best it can do is give the organisation a new start — which is worth something, but only if the underlying issues are addressed.

## What to invest in

If we're being honest with clients about where brand investment has the highest return, it's usually not the logo. It's the systems around it — the voice and tone guidelines that determine how every piece of communication sounds, the design system that makes every touchpoint feel coherent, the culture of craft that means people inside the organisation care about the details.

These are less glamorous than a rebrand reveal. They require sustained attention rather than a single creative push. But they're what actually builds a brand — the accumulated weight of consistent, considered expression across time.

## The logo still matters

We're not arguing against logos. We design a lot of them, and we care about doing it well. A strong mark gives a brand something to organise around, a visual anchor that works across contexts and endures across years.

But we'd rather our clients understood what they were buying when they commissioned one. Not a solution. A foundation. The work of building a brand starts with the logo and then continues, every day, in everything the organisation does.
