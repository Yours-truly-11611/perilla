There's a scene in every project where the client asks for more. More colour. More texture. More information. More assurance that people will notice.

It's an understandable instinct. You've spent months building something — a product, a service, a company — and now you're being asked to represent it in a wordmark and a colour palette and a typeface. The temptation to put everything in is almost physical.

Our job, at least half the time, is to resist that together.

## The case for less

The brands that last — the ones people can draw from memory, that work on a business card and a billboard, that look right in 2040 as they do today — share a quality of decisiveness. They've committed to a position. They've left things out.

This isn't minimalism as aesthetic preference. It's restraint as strategic act. Every element you add to a mark or a layout is an element competing for attention with every other element. The design that seems to "do more" often communicates less, because nothing within it has sufficient weight to be remembered.

## The client's fear

When a client asks for more, they're usually expressing a fear: that they won't be seen, that something important won't be communicated, that the design is leaving money on the table.

The answer to that fear isn't to add things. It's to make what's there more precise.

A well-chosen typeface communicates personality, authority, warmth, rigour — without a single additional element. A considered palette does the same. The work is in the choosing, not the accumulating.

## What we've learned to say

We've gotten better at naming the tension directly: "I understand the instinct to add, and I want to explain why I think it would cost us clarity." Then we look at precedents — not other brands in the client's sector, which often just normalise the clutter — but at things from outside: a piece of typography, a building, a film title sequence that demonstrates what decisive restraint looks like when it works.

The goal is for the client to feel the difference before we argue for it. Once you feel how much a well-composed silence communicates, it's hard to go back to noise.

## The honest version

None of this means we always get it right. We've made things that were too spare, that felt cold or incomplete, that left clients feeling undersold. Restraint without warmth is its own failure.

What we're after is sufficiency: the thing that has exactly what it needs and nothing it doesn't. Getting there requires more iteration than addition, more willingness to take things away than to put them in.

That's the work. It's slower and harder to explain. But the results — the things people still recognise twenty years on — make the argument better than we ever could.
