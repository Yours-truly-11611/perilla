We've turned down good money because the timeline was wrong.

This sounds principled when you say it, and I want to be honest: it's also been uncomfortable. There have been projects we wanted, clients we respected, work that would have been visible. And we've said no, or asked for more time, and sometimes lost the opportunity entirely.

What we've gained is a practice we're proud of.

## What speed costs

When a brand project runs in six weeks instead of sixteen, certain things don't happen. The strategy phase gets compressed. Research gives way to assumption. Early concepts become final concepts because there's no time to develop the alternatives that might have been better. The client approves something they feel okay about rather than something they love.

And the work goes into the world, and represents someone's business for the next five years, and it's fine. Not bad. Just not what it could have been.

We've made work like that, in the years when we were less confident about protecting time. We know the feeling of looking at something in the wild and thinking: we could have done better, if we'd had another three weeks.

## What slow makes possible

The best projects we've run have had long research phases — not because we were slow to move, but because we were deliberate about what we were moving toward.

In the Aurelius project, the discovery phase took four weeks. We did twelve stakeholder interviews. We wrote a forty-page strategic brief before we opened Illustrator. By the time we were in the design phase, we knew exactly what we were trying to make and why. The creative work went quickly because the thinking had been done.

That's what slow design actually means: front-loading the hard work, so the execution has clarity and confidence behind it.

## The honest conversation

Most clients, when we explain this, understand immediately. They've experienced the alternative — the rebrand that didn't quite land, the website that needed redoing in two years, the logo they're already slightly embarrassed about.

What they're often managing is internal pressure: a board that wants to see action, a launch date that's already announced, a budget cycle that doesn't allow for the right amount of time.

Our job, when that's the situation, is to help them understand what's possible in the available time and what isn't — and to make a genuine recommendation about how to proceed. Sometimes the answer is to do a smaller piece of work well, rather than a larger piece of work under-resourced.

We'd rather do the right-sized thing with integrity than the wrong-sized thing quickly.

## A note on craft

There's a reason the things we remember from any creative discipline were usually made slowly, with obsessive attention to detail. That's not romanticisation. It's how quality works. The eye for what's wrong, the willingness to start over, the patience to refine a curve or a rhythm until it's right — these things require time.

We're a small studio because we'd rather do fewer things at the level they deserve than more things at the level the market often accepts. That's a business decision, but it's also just what we believe.

The work is worth doing slowly. We intend to keep doing it that way.
