Craft is one of those words that's been stretched until it's nearly empty. Every agency claims it. Every pitch deck invokes it. We use it ourselves, and we want to be careful that we mean something specific when we do.

Here's our working definition: craft is the willingness to keep working on something after it's good enough.

Good enough, in design, is often quite good. A competent logo, a clean layout, a functional website — these things satisfy the brief. Craft is what happens after the brief is satisfied, when you're refining the kerning because the rhythm isn't quite right, when you're adjusting the colour because the first version was correct but not precise, when you're rewriting the headline because you found a better one at eleven o'clock on a Friday.

## What craft costs

It costs time, obviously. And time, in most studios, costs money that clients haven't paid for or that margins don't support.

This is the structural problem of craft in commercial design: the incentives often run against it. The difference between good enough and right is usually invisible to the person paying, at least in the short term. You can't always charge for the extra hour.

We've tried to build a practice where some of that time is simply part of how we work — absorbed into the way we price and the way we scope. Not every project can sustain it. But we try to protect the conditions that allow it on the projects that matter.

## What craft produces

The visible results of craft are often the things you don't notice. A logotype that reads correctly at every size. A layout that feels comfortable without you knowing why. A brand film that holds your attention without you registering how it's doing it.

Craft is felt before it's seen. It produces work that people describe as "considered" or "refined" without being able to say exactly where those qualities reside. They reside in the accumulated effect of many small decisions made correctly.

## The studio question

Whether a studio produces crafted work is ultimately a question of values, not capability. Most designers can do the extra hour if they think it matters. The question is whether the studio creates conditions where it matters to them — where the standard is set high enough that good enough doesn't feel like enough.

That's a culture question, and a leadership question. It requires being willing to tell people when something isn't there yet, even when it's already acceptable. It requires a kind of productive dissatisfaction.

We live in that dissatisfaction most of the time. We've learned to find it motivating rather than demoralising. It's why the work keeps getting better.
